# PCB layout — placement pass (2026-07-20)

`clock.kicad_pcb`, generated by `gen/pcb_build.py` from `clock.kicad_sch`
(same generated-not-hand-edited convention as the schematic — edit the
script, not the `.kicad_pcb`, and re-run to regenerate). Built directly on
KiCad's `pcbnew` Python API, run under KiCad's own bundled interpreter:

```
/Applications/KiCad/KiCad.app/Contents/Frameworks/Python.framework/Versions/3.9/bin/python3.9 gen/pcb_build.py
```

**Scope: placement + stackup + zones + keepouts + full net assignment.
No traces are routed.** All 182 schematic parts are placed, sided, and
carry their net (visible as ratsnest in the KiCad GUI); 216 nets are ready
to route by hand or push through an autorouter (File → Fabrication →
Freerouting export). Hand-routing ~200 nets across a 6-block, dual-rail,
motor+RF+audio board isn't something that converges to a *correct* result
without a router or a human at a screen — a first pass claiming to have
routed it would likely be wrong in ways nobody could see until fab. This
gets you the harder, judgment-heavy part (what goes where, and why) done
and verified; routing is mechanical from here.

## Stackup

4-layer, 1.6mm. **In1.Cu and In2.Cu are both solid GND pours** (outline
placed; fill computes on first open in the GUI or `Edit → Fill Zones` —
`ZONE_FILLER.Fill()` segfaults under the bundled scripting interpreter, so
this is deferred rather than baked in). Two GND planes rather than a single
split power plane: this board has six different rails (VBUS/BAT/+3V3/+5V/
+12V/PVDD) whose regions are already physically separated by the placement
grouping, so a hand-split power layer would mostly duplicate what
placement already gives you, while adding real risk of two differently-
named zones ending up too close together without a router in the loop to
guarantee clearance. GND-GND is also simply the better choice for a board
mixing an RF module, two stepper drivers, and a class-D-ish audio amp —
solid return path, easy shielding between F.Cu and B.Cu.

Board minimum clearance set to **0.1mm (4mil)** rather than KiCad's 0.2mm
default — needed by J1's native 0.5mm-pitch USB-C pads and U5's SOT-563
pads, both per their own datasheets, not a placement error. 0.1mm is
within PCBWay's advanced/HDI capability, not their base tier — flag this
when ordering.

## Placement method

135 of 182 parts (all R/C/RT plus most ICs/discretes) are **grid-packed**
into 8 rectangular per-block zones (`FILL_ZONES` in `pcb_build.py`), not
hand-coordinated — the schematic's own block structure (`b_*.py`
generators) gives the grouping, and a real bin-packer (tallest-first
shelf packing, sized from each footprint's actual measured pad geometry)
placed within a zone is more reliable than hand-picked mm offsets at this
component count. Only **4 parts have a genuine hand-placed anchor**: M1
(motor, must be dead centre), U8 (ESP32, antenna orientation), Y1 (crystal,
best-practice proximity to U8), and J6 (microSD, needs board-edge card
access). J3 (speaker) also got a specific hand position after the packer
first placed it directly under a front-side LED footprint — see below.

Every zone rectangle is checked (`verify_zones()`) against 3 hard
exclusions before anything is placed: the ESP32 keepout, the motor's
~19mm-radius courtyard (+1mm margin), and the battery footprint. This
replaced an earlier all-hand-coordinated ~54-anchor floor plan that looked
reasonable on paper and turned out to have 1000+ real overlaps once
actually checked — eyeballed mm-level spacing across 182 parts isn't
reliable without a live renderer in the loop; a verified packer is.

### Front-side decoupling caps (C230–236)

The schematic places one 100nF decoupling cap directly behind each of the
7 NeoPixels. Three of the bottom-arc LEDs (D41/D42/D43) sit, in XY, on top
of the battery holder's footprint on the back — completely fine for the
LEDs themselves (SMD on F.Cu, BT1's plastic body on B.Cu, no interference),
but a decoupling cap can't also live on B.Cu at that exact spot since
BT1's body is physically there. All 7 decoupling caps were moved to the
front instead, offset a few mm from their LED — cosmetically invisible
(hidden behind the dial/glass with the LEDs themselves) and simpler than
splitting the fix per-LED.

## Verification run

- `pcb_check.py` (custom, in `gen/`): pairwise courtyard-overlap, motor-
  keepout, and board-edge checks using each footprint's **pad geometry**,
  not `GetBoundingBox()` — the latter includes reference/value silkscreen
  text (some of it, e.g. `Fuse_TCO_Cantherm_SDF_L10.5mm_...`, 50+ characters)
  and produced hundreds of false-positive "overlaps" before this was
  caught. Final state: 0 keepout violations, 0 board-edge overflows, 6
  same-side "overlaps" — all BT1 (back) vs. front LEDs/caps at the same
  XY, which is the expected double-sided-board case described above, not
  a defect.
- `kicad-cli pcb drc --severity-all`: 0 clearance violations, 0 real
  shorts introduced by placement. Remaining flagged items:
  - **398 silk_overlap / silk_over_copper** — reference-designator text
    reflowing after dense packing; cosmetic, fixed in five minutes in the
    GUI (Tools → Cleanup, or manual nudge), zero effect on fabrication.
  - **13 shorting_items + 12 drill_out_of_range**, all on U8 pad "41" —
    the stock ESP32-S3-WROOM-1 footprint's exposed-pad/thermal-via array
    reports a duplicate pad number the schematic didn't net-map cleanly.
    Pre-existing footprint/schematic pairing quirk, not a placement
    artifact — needs a schematic-side fix (map the EP pin to GND
    explicitly), not a PCB one.
  - **4 pth_inside_courtyard** — J7/J10/J11 (all small JST connectors in
    the IO_SENSOR zone) placed closer than their courtyard margins prefer.
    Electrically fine (0 clearance violations among them); would benefit
    from a little more breathing room in a routing pass.

## Regenerating

```
cd gen
/Applications/KiCad/KiCad.app/Contents/Frameworks/Python.framework/Versions/3.9/bin/python3.9 pcb_build.py   # rebuild placement
/Applications/KiCad/KiCad.app/Contents/Frameworks/Python.framework/Versions/3.9/bin/python3.9 pcb_check.py   # overlap/keepout re-check
/Applications/KiCad/KiCad.app/Contents/MacOS/kicad-cli pcb drc --severity-all clock.kicad_pcb                 # full DRC
```

`pcb_build.py` re-derives the BOM straight from `clock.kicad_sch` every
run (never hand-transcribed), so schematic edits (new parts, footprint
swaps) flow through on the next build — you'd only need to touch
`ANCHORS` / `FILL_ZONES` if a *new* part needs a home.
